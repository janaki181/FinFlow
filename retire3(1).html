Retirement Schemes Comparison

Explore different retirement plans and schemes to find the best fit for your financial situation and goals.

401(k) Plans

Employer-sponsored retirement plans with potential matching contributions. Contributions are tax-deferred, reducing your current taxable income.

  • 2024 Contribution limit: $23,000
  • Catch-up contributions (age 50+): $7,500
  • Tax-deferred growth until withdrawal

Individual Retirement Accounts (IRAs)

Personal retirement accounts with different tax advantages depending on the type (Traditional or Roth).

  • 2024 Contribution limit: $7,000
  • Catch-up contributions (age 50+): $1,000
  • Traditional: Tax-deductible contributions
  • Roth: Tax-free qualified withdrawals

Health Savings Accounts (HSAs)

Triple tax advantage accounts that can be used as retirement savings vehicles when paired with high-deductible health plans.

  • 2024 Contribution limit: $4,150 (individual), $8,300 (family)
  • Catch-up contributions (age 55+): $1,000
  • Tax-deductible contributions, tax-free growth, and tax-free withdrawals for qualified medical expenses

Frequently Asked Questions

Get answers to common questions about retirement planning and management.

How much money do I need to retire comfortably?

The amount needed varies based on your desired lifestyle, location, and health status. A common guideline is to aim for 70-80% of your pre-retirement income. Our retirement calculator can help you determine a more personalized target based on your specific situation.

When should I start taking Social Security benefits?

You can start taking Social Security benefits as early as age 62, but your benefits increase the longer you wait, up to age 70. The optimal time depends on your health, life expectancy, other sources of income, and financial needs. Taking benefits at your full retirement age (66-67 for most people) provides your full benefit amount.

How do I protect my retirement savings from market downturns?

Diversification is key to protecting your retirement savings. As you approach retirement, gradually shift to a more conservative asset allocation with a higher percentage of bonds and cash equivalents. Consider maintaining 2-3 years of expenses in cash or cash equivalents to avoid selling investments during market downturns. Some retirees also use annuities to provide guaranteed income regardless of market performance.

What healthcare options are available in retirement?

For most Americans, Medicare becomes available at age 65. This includes Medicare Part A (hospital insurance) and Part B (medical insurance). Many retirees also purchase Medicare Supplement Insurance (Medigap) to cover costs not covered by Medicare. Medicare Part D covers prescription drugs. Long-term care insurance is another option to consider for potential nursing home or in-home care needs. If retiring before 65, you'll need to secure private health insurance until Medicare eligibility.

How does inflation affect my retirement planning?

Inflation erodes purchasing power over time, making goods and services more expensive. For retirement planning, it's essential to factor in an inflation rate (typically 2-3% annually) when calculating future expenses. To combat inflation, maintain some growth investments in your portfolio even after retirement, and consider inflation-protected securities like TIPS. Social Security benefits include cost-of-living adjustments, providing some protection against inflation.
